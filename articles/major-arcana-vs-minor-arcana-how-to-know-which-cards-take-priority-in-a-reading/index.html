Major Arcana vs. Minor Arcana: How to Know Which Cards Take Priority in a Reading

When I first started pulling cards, I felt like I was staring at a scattershot of images. One minute I was looking at The Fool, and the next, I was holding the Three of Pentacles. I spent too much time trying to weigh them equally, treating every card as if it had the same “volume” in the conversation.

I quickly realized that isn’t how a reading works. If you treat every card as a headline, you end up with a noisy, confusing narrative. To get clear answers, you have to learn how to identify the “big picture” cards and the “day-to-day” cards. That is the fundamental difference between the Major Arcana and the Minor Arcana.

The Major Arcana: The Life-Altering Events

Think of the Major Arcana as the “life path” cards. There are 22 of them, numbered 0 through 21. When these show up in a spread, they aren’t talking about your commute or whether your boss is annoyed with you. They are talking about your identity, major milestones, and karmic shifts.

If you are doing a three-card spread for a career question and you pull The Tower, don’t waste time wondering if you should update your resume. The Tower suggests a structural collapse of your current situation—something is ending abruptly to make room for a new foundation. It is a loud, intrusive card.

When I see a reading dominated by Major Arcana, I know the person is in a period of intense personal transformation. The advice here is rarely “do this small task” and almost always “prepare for a change in perspective.”

The Minor Arcana: The Daily Mechanics

The remaining 56 cards are the Minor Arcana. These are divided into four suits—Wands, Cups, Swords, and Pentacles—which map onto the Four Elements (Fire, Water, Air, and Earth).

If the Major Arcana are the weather report, the Minor Arcana are the clothes you choose to wear that day. They deal with the specific, practical details of your life.

  • Wands: Your energy, passions, and creative sparks.
  • Cups: Your emotional landscape and relationships.
  • Swords: Your thought processes, conflicts, and communication.
  • Pentacles: Your physical world, money, health, and routine.

When a spread is heavy with Minor Arcana, it tells me the situation is fluid. You have agency here. These cards are “fixable.” If you pull the Seven of Swords, you might be dealing with a minor misunderstanding or a need to be more strategic at work. It’s a call to action, not a prediction of fate.

How to Weigh Them in a Reading

The easiest way to prioritize your reading is to count how many cards from each group landed on the table.

If I pull seven cards and five of them are from the Major Arcana, I ignore the “background noise” of the Minor cards. The Major cards are the priority; they are the primary force at play. The Minor cards in that spread are simply the “side effects” of the bigger transition.

Conversely, if I pull eight cards and seven are from the Minor Arcana, I stop looking for grand life themes. I focus entirely on the practical steps. I look for the suit that is most prominent. If there are four Swords cards, the issue isn’t money or love—it’s an intellectual conflict or a need to communicate more clearly.

A Practical Example: The Job Search

Imagine you ask, “Should I take this new job offer?”

Scenario A: You pull The Chariot (Major Arcana) and the Ace of Pentacles (Minor Arcana).

  • Priority: The Chariot. This is a card of movement and overcoming obstacles through willpower. It suggests the job is a major step forward in your personal journey.
  • Secondary: The Ace of Pentacles. This provides the “how.” It indicates the job will offer a solid financial start. The reading tells you to move forward because it aligns with your life’s momentum.

Scenario B: You pull the Five of Cups (Minor) and the Eight of Swords (Minor).

  • Priority: Both are Minor. There is no Major Arcana “anchor.”
  • The Message: You are dealing with a mental hurdle (Swords) and a regretful attachment to the past (Cups). The job itself isn’t the issue; your hesitation is. You aren’t being asked to make a life-changing destiny move; you are being asked to manage your emotions and clear your head before making a decision.

Avoiding Analysis Paralysis

One mistake beginners make is trying to find a “Major Arcana meaning” in a Minor Arcana card. If you pull the Three of Cups, don’t try to make it about your soul’s ultimate purpose. It’s about friendship, celebration, and social connection. Keep it grounded.

When you sit down to interpret your spread, scan for the Major Arcana first. Ask yourself if the situation is a permanent shift or a temporary hurdle. Once you identify the “heaviness” of the reading, you can stop stressing over the small stuff and focus on the cards that actually hold the weight of the answer.

Understanding this balance allows you to stop reading every card as a life-or-death prophecy and start using the deck as a practical decision-making tool. Once you identify which cards are setting the stage and which are just the actors, you can effectively plan your next steps.
