How to Interpret Major Arcana Cards When They Appear in a Three-Card Spread

When you pull a three-card spread, you are usually looking for a narrative: where you’ve been, where you are, and where you are going. Most people start by reading the Minor Arcana, which represent the day-to-day fluctuations of life. But when a Major Arcana card lands in your spread, the energy shifts. These cards—the ones numbered 0 through 21—don’t represent small habits or daily moods. They represent the “big stuff”: karmic lessons, life transitions, and archetypal forces that are much larger than your current situation.

If you are just starting out, seeing a Major Arcana card can feel intimidating. Here is how I approach them when they appear in a standard three-card layout.

Identifying the Weight of the Card

In a three-card spread, position matters. If you are using a standard “Past, Present, Future” layout, a Major Arcana card acting as the “Present” is a signal that you are currently in the middle of a major life pivot.

For example, if you pull The Tower in the middle position, it is not suggesting that you have a bad attitude about your job; it is signaling that the foundation of your current situation is undergoing a forced, necessary collapse. If you see this, stop looking for minor solutions. A Major Arcana card in the center tells you that the “small fix” approach won’t work anymore. You are dealing with an unavoidable external change.

When I see a Major card, I immediately ask myself: “Is this card bringing a lesson I need to learn, or a change I cannot stop?” This distinction helps you stop trying to control the uncontrollable and start focusing on your internal reaction to it.

The “Major” Influence on Minor Cards

One of the most useful tricks for reading these spreads is to treat the Major Arcana card as the “director” of the scene, while the Minor Arcana cards are the “supporting actors.”

Imagine you pull The Hermit, The Eight of Pentacles, and The Page of Swords.

  • The Hermit (Major) is the theme. It demands withdrawal, introspection, and solitude.
  • The Eight of Pentacles (Minor) is the craft or work you are doing.
  • The Page of Swords (Minor) is the mental energy or communication style.

Instead of reading these as three separate events, the Major Arcana card forces you to view the others through its lens. You aren’t just working hard (Eight of Pentacles) and thinking critically (Page of Swords); you are doing those things specifically because you are in a period of necessary isolation (The Hermit). The Major card provides the “why.” If you ignore the Major card, you’ll miss the reason behind your current actions.

Dealing with Multiple Major Arcana Cards

Sometimes, you will pull two or three Major Arcana cards in a single spread. This happens more often than beginners expect. When your entire three-card pull is composed of Major Arcana, you are no longer looking at a “situation.” You are looking at a “cycle.”

A spread of The Chariot, Death, and The World is a heavy hitter. This isn’t about your Tuesday afternoon. This is about a complete life transformation. When you see this, don’t try to break it down into minor, tactical steps. Instead, look at the sequence. The Chariot suggests you are moving forward with force, Death suggests the ending of a long-held identity, and The World suggests you are closing a massive chapter of your life.

When you get a spread like this, treat it as a directive to stop worrying about the small details. The big picture is moving faster than your daily plans can keep up with. In this case, your best strategy is surrender rather than strategy.

Practical Steps to Take Today

If you have a reading in front of you right now, use this checklist to see if you are giving the Major Arcana cards the attention they deserve:

  • Check the numerical order: Are the Major cards in a sequence (like The Lovers followed by The Chariot)? If so, the spread is telling a story of progression. If they are out of order, the spread might be highlighting a conflict between where you’ve been and where you want to go.
  • Look for the “Anchor”: Identify the Major Arcana card in your spread. Ask yourself, “If this card were a person, what advice would they give to the other two cards?” This helps personify the archetypal energy.
  • Assess the “Volume”: If the Minor Arcana cards are quiet (like the Four of Cups), but the Major Arcana card is loud (like The Devil), the Major card is screaming. It is telling you that your current state of “boredom” or “apathy” is actually a trap you need to address immediately.

Moving Forward

Reading the Major Arcana doesn’t require memorizing complex occult history. It requires recognizing that some moments in life are meant to be experienced as “big lessons” rather than “minor tasks.” Once you start grouping your cards by their influence, the narrative of your reading will become much clearer. You stop seeing a random collection of images and start seeing the roadmap of your current trajectory.

As you reflect on these cards, consider how they interact with your current environment and the specific goals you are working toward. If you are ready to dig deeper into your personal spread and want to understand how these archetypes are playing out in your specific life path, the next steps involve looking at how the individual suits in your deck support the major transitions identified by these powerful cards.
