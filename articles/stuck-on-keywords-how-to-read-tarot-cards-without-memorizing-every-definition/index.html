When I first started reading tarot, I spent weeks carrying around a tiny, dog-eared guidebook. I was convinced that if I didn’t recite the exact dictionary definition for the Five of Pentacles or the Queen of Swords, I was “doing it wrong.” I’d lay out a spread, look at a card, and flip frantically through the pages. The flow was broken, my intuition was silent, and honestly, I was just reading a book to myself rather than performing a reading.

If you feel like you need a photographic memory to start reading cards, I have good news: you don’t. In fact, relying on rigid definitions can actually prevent you from seeing what the cards are trying to say in the context of your specific life. Here is how you can move away from rote memorization and start reading with confidence today.

Learn the “Story” of the Suits

Instead of memorizing 78 individual meanings, start by understanding the four suits of the Minor Arcana. These are the building blocks of every card. If you know the “vibe” of the suit, you can guess the meaning of almost any card in that category even if you’ve never seen it before.

  • Cups (Water): These cards are about your emotional world, relationships, and how you feel about a situation. If you pull a Cup, ask yourself: “How is my heart involved here?”
  • Pentacles (Earth): These deal with the physical, tangible world. Think money, career, health, and your immediate environment. If you pull a Pentacle, ask: “What is the practical, grounded outcome?”
  • Swords (Air): These represent the mind, logic, communication, and conflict. Swords are the “head” cards. If you pull one, ask: “What is the truth I’m avoiding or the decision I need to make?”
  • Wands (Fire): These are about passion, inspiration, creativity, and energy. Wands are the “action” cards. If you pull one, ask: “Where do I need to take a risk or ignite a new project?”

Once you categorize the card by its element, you’ve already done half the work. Even if you forget the “official” meaning, you know if the reading is leaning toward a practical problem (Pentacles) or an emotional one (Cups).

Use Visual Anchors

Stop looking at the guidebook and start looking at the art. Tarot decks are designed with visual cues that provide clues to the meaning. Even if you aren’t an art critic, you can use basic observation to build a narrative.

Let’s say you pull the Three of Swords—the card with the heart pierced by three blades. You don’t need a book to tell you that this looks painful. It looks like a betrayal or a stinging disappointment. Now, look at the Two of Pentacles. You see a figure juggling two coins while standing on a wavy surface. It looks like someone trying to keep their balance while life shifts around them.

When you practice, ask yourself these three questions:

  1. What is the main action happening in the image? (Is someone fighting, resting, walking away, or offering something?)
  2. How does the color palette feel? (Is it bright and yellow, suggesting optimism, or dark and blue, suggesting introspection?)
  3. What is the facial expression of the figure on the card?

If you answer these three questions, you’ll find that you have a coherent sentence about the card without ever having to look up a definition.

Create a “Three-Card” Context Loop

One of the biggest mistakes beginners make is trying to read each card in isolation. Instead, try to build a sentence. If you draw three cards, don’t read them as “Card A, Card B, Card C.” Read them as a story: “Because of [Card A], I am feeling [Card B], which leads to [Card C].”

For example, if you pull the Ace of Wands (a spark of new energy), the Five of Pentacles (a sense of lack or financial worry), and the Eight of Pentacles (hard work and skill-building), you don’t need to look up the exact definitions. You can interpret that as: “I have a new, exciting idea, but I’m currently worried about my resources. The cards are telling me that the way to fix this is to put my head down and work on my craft.”

Build Your Own Reference Sheet

Instead of a 300-page book, create a one-page “cheat sheet” based on your own reactions to the cards. Take your deck and go through it card by card. Write down one word that pops into your head when you see the image—not what the book says, but what you feel.

For the Tower card, maybe you write “chaos.” For the Lovers, maybe you write “alignment.” If you do this once for all 78 cards, you will have created a personal dictionary that is far more accurate for your reading style than any mass-produced manual. You are building a language you actually speak, rather than memorizing a dictionary in a language you don’t use.

Trusting Your First Impression

Your brain is incredibly good at pattern recognition. When you turn a card over, your first instinct—the one that happens in the first two seconds—is almost always the correct one. If you ignore that feeling and start digging through a book, you are actively suppressing your own ability to interpret the symbols.

The next time you do a reading, leave the guidebook in another room. Pull three cards, look at the pictures, and tell a story based on your gut. You will find that you already know far more than you give yourself credit for, and the “right” answer was already waiting in your own perspective.
