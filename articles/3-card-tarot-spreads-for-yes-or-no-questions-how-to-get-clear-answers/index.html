When I first started reading tarot, I quickly realized that asking a simple “yes or no” question usually resulted in a cryptic mess. If I pulled a single card, I often felt confused. Did the card mean “yes, but it will be hard,” or “no, because you aren’t ready”?

Using a three-card spread for a binary question changed everything for me. It adds nuance, highlights the obstacles, and gives you a clear direction. Instead of just getting a flat answer, you get a mini-map of how that answer manifests in your life.

Why Three Cards Beat One

When you pull a single card for a yes/no question, you are often left guessing about the “why.” If you pull the Three of Swords, is it a “no” because of heartbreak, or a warning to prepare for it?

By using three cards, you create a narrative arc. I typically arrange them from left to right. The first card represents the current energy of the situation, the second card acts as the “yes” or “no” indicator, and the third card serves as the advice or outcome. This structure forces you to look at the context rather than just the verdict.

How to Set Up Your Spread

Before you shuffle, you need to define your positions. If you don’t set the rules before the cards hit the table, you will end up projecting your own desires onto the results.

Here is the framework I use for almost every binary decision:

  • Card 1 (The Root): What is fueling this question right now? This identifies your underlying motive.
  • Card 2 (The Verdict): The primary answer. If it’s a Major Arcana card, treat it as a strong, non-negotiable answer. If it’s a Minor Arcana card, treat it as a situational or temporary answer.
  • Card 3 (The Advice): How should you move forward based on the verdict? This tells you what to do next to either secure the “yes” or mitigate the “no.”

For example, let’s say you ask, “Should I take that new job?” You pull the Seven of Pentacles for the verdict. That’s a “yes,” but it’s a conditional one—it implies that you will need to put in a lot of time before you see the payoff. It’s not an instant “yes,” and the third card will likely tell you whether that long-term investment is actually worth your energy.

Assigning Values to the Suits

To keep your readings consistent, it helps to view the suits through the lens of a binary answer. When I am looking for a quick “yes” or “no,” I use the following as my baseline:

  • Wands: Usually a “yes,” provided you have the passion and energy to drive it forward.
  • Cups: A “yes,” but only if your emotions are aligned with the decision. If the card is reversed, it’s a “no” driven by insecurity.
  • Swords: Often a “no” or a “slow down.” Swords represent intellect and logic; they usually suggest that your head is getting in the way of your intuition, or that the timing isn’t right.
  • Pentacles: Usually a “yes” regarding work, money, or physical health. If the card is a low number, it’s a “yes” but you need to do the groundwork first.

Dealing with Ambiguous Results

Sometimes, even after pulling three cards, you still feel stuck. This happens when the cards don’t clearly lean toward a positive or negative outcome.

If you pull three cards and get a mix of, say, the Fool (new beginnings), the Five of Wands (conflict), and the Eight of Cups (walking away), the universe is likely telling you that you are asking the wrong question. In this case, the cards are telling you that “yes” or “no” isn’t the point. You are missing a piece of information, or your current approach is too scattered.

When this happens, put the cards away. Don’t keep pulling until you get the answer you want—that is the fastest way to lose the clarity you’re looking for. Instead, go back to the drawing board and rephrase your question. Ask “What do I need to know about this situation?” instead of “Should I do this?”

Practical Tips for Clearer Readings

If you want to move from “guessing” to “knowing,” follow these three rules:

  1. Be specific: Don’t ask, “Will I be happy?” Ask, “Will this specific decision improve my daily routine?” The more concrete your question, the more concrete the cards will be.
  2. Use a journal: Write down the date, the question, and the three cards. Come back a week later. You will start to notice patterns in how certain cards answer your specific types of questions.
  3. Trust your first reaction: The moment you flip that middle card (the Verdict), you will have an immediate gut feeling. That split-second intuition is often more accurate than any guidebook. The rest of the reading is just there to help you process why you feel that way.

Moving Forward

Once you have your three-card spread laid out, take a moment to look at how the cards interact. Do they share a color palette? Are there recurring symbols? These small details bridge the gap between a generic reading and a personal insight.
